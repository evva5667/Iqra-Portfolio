Crip Camp Video

Watching "Crip Camp: A Disability Revolution" left me speechless. The documentary was both emotional and powerful, and I can't thank you enough for recommending it. The concept of Camp Jened was revolutionary at the time, and it's fascinating to see how the campers, who later became pioneers of the disability rights movement, were instrumental in driving such monumental change.

What struck me most was the enduring camaraderie among the campers. They demonstrated to the world that their disabilities wouldn't stop them from making significant contributions, even going as far as to ensure legislation was passed to protect their rights. The sense of fulfillment and achievement in their eyes when Section 504 was passed was truly moving. I think the documentary does an excellent job of portraying these individuals simply as people. A moment that particularly resonated with me was when John asked Michael Tannenbaum what he loved about Camp Jened. Michael's response‚Äîthat the staff didn't treat them like they were babysitting them, unlike other camps‚Äîhighlighted the respect and dignity afforded to them at Jened, which was fundamentally different from their experiences elsewhere.

The activists' brilliance was also highlighted by their ability to bring Manhattan to a standstill by blocking a single street. This determination was really inspiring. Although I've never participated in a demonstration, seeing their steadfast resolve was a lesson in the power of advocacy, I loved it. I remember while watching it one interviewee's comment really stuck with me: "We want people who will not accept 'no' without asking 'why.'" And I think this philosophy is such a powerful call to action, reminding me of the importance of persistence and inquisitive engagement, like they know what they are asking for.

There were also incredible instances of resourcefulness and solidarity which were really emotional for me to watch them. For example, when the FBI cut off their telephone connections, the response from the deaf community was ingenious. And the story of the individual who improvised a refrigerator using just cardboard and an air conditioner is a testament to the innovative spirit that permeated their movement.

I would say in short, this documentary is more than just a film; it's a compelling reminder of the impact that a united group of people can have on society, challenging us to question, to support, and to act. And made me wonder are those people disable or the world were have created is not able enough to make their lives easier.

Accessibility to digital Technology: Virtual barriers and real opportunities

One key takeaway from the article about digital technology accessibility is that the true barrier for people with disabilities often lies in the proprietary and closed nature of the underlying protocols and standards. This understanding didn't come to me immediately; it emerged as I delved deeper into recent updates and developments in the tech world, revealing more systemic issues.

A recurring problem in digital technology‚Äîspanning operating systems, applications, and websites‚Äîis that while initial accessibility issues might be addressed, they frequently resurface with every new update or version. I think when iOS 13 was released in September 2019, it introduced bugs that disrupted VoiceOver, touch sensitivity, and Bluetooth connectivity for accessibility devices, causing issues for users with disabilities. After receiving feedback from the disability community, Apple quickly addressed these issues in updates starting with iOS 13.1.3, restoring functionality and improving the overall user experience for affected individuals. Its like a garden with a pathway specifically designed for wheelchair access. Initially, the pathway is wide and clear, accommodating everyone. However, now someone decided to upgrade the garden, and the workers, focusing on aesthetics rather than function, inadvertently place decorations that narrow the path, obstructing wheelchair access.

This garden is like a software and digital products. The pathway's initial design was accessible, but without ongoing oversight focused on maintaining this accessibility‚Äîakin to the garden designer's original intent‚Äîupgrades can reintroduce barriers. In the digital world, these upgrades are software updates that may not be overseen by those who are mindful of accessibility needs.

I like how author highlights that digital accessibility issues tend to re-emerge because the development process itself often doesn't prioritize accessibility as a fundamental component. It might be that a capitalist mindset is at play here, where companies prioritize updates and features that cater to the majority of their consumers who don't require accessibility options, leading to less attention on making products accessible for all.

Just to keep it short now, I think reflection has not only helped me understand the complexities of digital accessibility but also the importance of viewing technology development as an ongoing process that should inclusively cater to all users.

Project Overview

Afternoon Nap follows Mahlet as she drifts into a dream that feels oddly familiar - an ordinary day with her roommate Naz slowly unravels into something more emotionally charged. What begins with a small, seemingly harmless habit becomes the trigger for a deeper inner conflict, placing viewers in control of how Mahlet responds.

Created detailed storyboards and flowcharts to map the interactive narrative structure. Designed wireframes for the web experience and planned the technical requirements.

Filming

Used BlackMagic Pocket Camera 4K with creative techniques including a trolley dolly system. Employed varied camera movements from static tripod shots to dynamic handheld sequences.

Post-Production

Edited in DaVinci Resolve with custom title sequences, color correction, and audio mixing. Integrated voiceovers and external sound elements including opera music.

Challenges & Solutions

Technical Challenges

Working with 80GB+ of 4K ProRes footage caused performance issues in DaVinci Resolve. We solved this by generating proxy files for editing while maintaining high-resolution exports.

Creative Solutions

Used a dormitory trolley as a makeshift dolly for smooth camera movements. Coordinated team movements and placed soft fabric under the tripod to reduce vibrations.

User testing was crucial in refining the interactive elements.
